3.

Calculus
Prove the Basel problem, showing that the infinite sum of the reciprocals of the squares of the natural numbers equals pi squared over six:
$$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6} $$
Using Euler's original approach, assume that the infinite polynomial expansion of the sine function can be factored in terms of its roots, just like a finite polynomial.
Hint: Consider the Maclaurin series expansion of $\frac{\sin x}{x}$ and compare the coefficient of $x^2$ with its infinite product representation (the Weierstrass factorization).
Solution: Equate the $x^2$ coefficient of the Taylor series $\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{3!} + \frac{x^4}{5!} - \dots$ with the expanded infinite product $\prod (1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2\pi^2})$. The coefficient of $x^2$ in the infinite product is $-\sum \frac{1}{n^2\pi^2}$. Setting this equal to $-\frac{1}{3!}$ yields the final result.

2.

Prove that there are infinitely many primes $p$ such that $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$...

Number Theory
Prove that there are infinitely many primes $p$ such that:
$$ p \equiv 1 \pmod 4 $$
You may want to use a proof by contradiction similar to Euclid's classic proof for the infinitude of all primes, but utilizing quadratic residues.
Hint: Assume there are finitely many such primes, $p_1, \dots, p_k$. Consider the prime factors of the constructed number $N = (2p_1 \dots p_k)^2 + 1$.
Solution: Any odd prime factor $q$ of $N$ must satisfy $(2p_1 \dots p_k)^2 \equiv -1 \pmod q$. This implies $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo $q$. By properties of the Legendre symbol, this means $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. Since $q$ divides $N$, it cannot be any of the original $p_i$, contradicting the assumption that the list was finite.

1.

Linear Algebra
Let $A$ and $B$ be $n \times n$ matrices with real entries. Prove that the trace operator is cyclically commutative:
$$ \text{tr}(AB) = \text{tr}(BA) $$
Do not use the property of eigenvalues, but rather prove it directly using the index summation definition of matrix multiplication.
Hint: Write out the explicit definition for the trace: $\text{tr}(M) = \sum_{i=1}^n M_{ii}$. Substitute the definition of matrix multiplication for $M = AB$.
Solution: By definition, $\text{tr}(AB) = \sum_i (AB)_{ii} = \sum_i \sum_j A_{ij}B_{ji}$. Because scalar multiplication is commutative in the real numbers, we can rearrange the terms and sums: $\sum_j \sum_i B_{ji}A_{ij} = \sum_j (BA)_{jj} = \text{tr}(BA)$.
